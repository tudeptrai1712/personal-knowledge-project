Limits 101: The Foundation of Calculus

An AP-level overview of limits.

What is a Limit?

A limit is the value that a function "approaches" as the input "approaches" some value. Limits are essential to calculus as they are used to define continuity, derivatives, and integrals. The core idea is about getting arbitrarily close to a point, without actually being at that point.

We write the limit of a function $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $c$ as:

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L $$

This means that the value of $f(x)$ can be made as close as we want to $L$ by choosing $x$ sufficiently close to $c$.

Finding Limits Analytically

Several methods can be used to find limits:

  • Direct Substitution: If the function is continuous at $x=c$, we can often just plug in $c$ to find the limit. For example, $\lim_{x \to 2} (x^2 + 1) = 2^2 + 1 = 5$.
  • Factoring and Canceling: If direct substitution results in an indeterminate form like $\frac{0}{0}$, we can often factor the numerator and denominator and cancel common factors.
  • Rationalizing: For functions involving radicals, multiplying the numerator and denominator by the conjugate can help resolve indeterminate forms.

One-Sided Limits

A limit can also be approached from one side.

  • The limit from the left, $\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x)$, considers only values of $x$ less than $c$.
  • The limit from the right, $\lim_{x \to c^+} f(x)$, considers only values of $x$ greater than $c$.

A two-sided limit $\lim_{x \to c} f(x)$ exists if and only if both the left and right-sided limits exist and are equal.

Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes

An infinite limit occurs when the function's values increase or decrease without bound as $x$ approaches a certain number.

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = \infty \quad \text{or} \quad \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = -\infty $$

When this happens, the line $x=c$ is a vertical asymptote of the graph of $f(x)$. This often occurs in rational functions where the denominator is zero at $x=c$ but the numerator is not.

Limits at Infinity and Horizontal Asymptotes

We can also examine the end behavior of a function by looking at its limit at infinity.

$$ \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L \quad \text{or} \quad \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = M $$

If such a limit $L$ or $M$ exists, it means the function approaches a specific value as $x$ gets very large (or very small). The lines $y=L$ and $y=M$ are horizontal asymptotes. For rational functions, these can be found by comparing the degrees of the numerator and denominator.

Continuity and The Squeeze Theorem

A function $f$ is continuous at a point $c$ if $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$. This means the limit exists, the function is defined at that point, and the two values are equal.

The Squeeze Theorem is a powerful tool used to find the limit of a function that is "squeezed" between two other functions that have the same limit at a given point.
