Limits 101: The Foundation of Calculus

An AP-level overview of limits.

What is a Limit?

A limit is the value that a function "approaches" as the input "approaches" some value. Limits are essential to calculus as they are used to define continuity, derivatives, and integrals. The core idea is about getting arbitrarily close to a point, without actually being at that point.

We write the limit of a function $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $c$ as:

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L $$

This means that the value of $f(x)$ can be made as close as we want to $L$ by choosing $x$ sufficiently close to $c$.

Finding Limits Analytically

  • Direct Substitution: If the function is continuous at $x=c$, we can often just plug in $c$. Example: $\lim_{x \to 2} (x^2 + 1) = 5$.
  • Factoring and Canceling: For indeterminate forms like $0/0$, factor numerator and denominator to simplify.
  • Rationalizing: For radicals, multiply by the conjugate to simplify.

One-Sided Limits

  • Left-hand limit: $\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x)$
  • Right-hand limit: $\lim_{x \to c^+} f(x)$

A two-sided limit exists if both one-sided limits exist and are equal.

Infinite Limits & Vertical Asymptotes

When a function grows without bound near $x=c$:

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = \infty \quad \text{or} \quad -\infty $$

Here, $x=c$ is a vertical asymptote.

Limits at Infinity & Horizontal Asymptotes

End behavior is captured via limits at infinity:

$$ \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L, \quad \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = M $$

If these exist, the lines $y=L$ and $y=M$ are horizontal asymptotes.

Continuity and The Squeeze Theorem

A function $f$ is continuous at $c$ if $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$. The Squeeze Theorem helps find limits for functions trapped between two other functions with the same limit.
