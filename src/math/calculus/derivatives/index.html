Derivatives 101: The Complete Guide

An AP-level overview of derivatives in calculus.

What is a Derivative?

The derivative of a function measures the instantaneous rate of change of the function. Geometrically, the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line to the function's graph at that point. It's one of the two fundamental concepts of calculus, alongside the integral.

The Limit Definition of a Derivative

The derivative of a function $f(x)$, denoted as $f'(x)$, is formally defined using limits:

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} $$

This formula calculates the slope of the secant line between two points on the curve as the distance between them, $h$, approaches zero. This limit, if it exists, gives the precise slope of the tangent line at point $x$.

Basic Differentiation Rules

To avoid using the limit definition every time, we use a set of differentiation rules.

  • The Power Rule: If $f(x) = x^n$, then $f'(x) = n \cdot x^{n-1}$.
  • The Constant Rule: If $f(x) = c$ (a constant), then $f'(x) = 0$.
  • The Sum/Difference Rule: The derivative of a sum or difference is the sum or difference of the derivatives: $(f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'$.

The Product and Quotient Rules

For more complex functions, we have:

  • The Product Rule: For two functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$, the derivative of their product is:

    $$ (f(x)g(x))' = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x) $$

  • The Quotient Rule: The derivative of their quotient is:

    $$ \left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right)' = \frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} $$

The Chain Rule

The Chain Rule is used to differentiate composite functions. If we have a function $h(x) = f(g(x))$, its derivative is:

$$ h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) $$

In words: "the derivative of the outer function (with the inside function unchanged) times the derivative of the inner function."

Derivatives of Trigonometric Functions

Key trigonometric derivatives include:

  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x$

Implicit Differentiation

When a function is not explicitly written as $y = f(x)$ (e.g., $x^2 + y^2 = 1$), we use implicit differentiation. This involves differentiating both sides of the equation with respect to $x$ and then solving for $\frac{dy}{dx}$. Remember to use the chain rule for terms involving $y$.

Higher-Order Derivatives

The second derivative, $f''(x)$, is the derivative of the first derivative. It describes the concavity of a function. The third derivative, $f'''(x)$, is the derivative of the second derivative, and so on.

  • If $f''(x) > 0$, the function is concave up.
  • If $f''(x) < 0$, the function is concave down.
