Integrals 101: The Complete Guide

An AP-level overview of integrals in calculus.

What is an Integral?

The integral is the second fundamental concept of calculus. It can be understood in two main ways: as an indefinite integral, which represents the reverse process of differentiation (an antiderivative), and as a definite integral, which represents the accumulated area under the curve of a function between two points.

Indefinite Integrals and Antiderivatives

An antiderivative of a function $f(x)$ is a function $F(x)$ whose derivative is $f(x)$. That is, $F'(x) = f(x)$. The indefinite integral is the family of all antiderivatives of $f(x)$, written as:

$$ \int f(x) \,dx = F(x) + C $$

The $C$ is the constant of integration, which is necessary because the derivative of any constant is zero. For example, since the derivative of $x^2$ is $2x$, the indefinite integral of $2x$ is $\int 2x \,dx = x^2 + C$.

The Definite Integral

The definite integral of a function $f(x)$ from $a$ to $b$ is the net area under the curve of $f(x)$ between these two points. It is written as:

$$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \,dx $$

Conceptually, this is the limit of a sum of the areas of an infinite number of rectangles (a Riemann Sum) under the curve.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This theorem links the concepts of differentiation and integration.

  • Part 1: If $g(x) = \int_{a}^{x} f(t) \,dt$, then $g'(x) = f(x)$. This means the derivative of an integral function is the original function.
  • Part 2: If $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ (i.e., $F' = f$), then the definite integral can be easily evaluated:

    $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \,dx = F(b) - F(a) $$

Basic Integration Rules

Many integration rules are the reverse of differentiation rules.

  • Reverse Power Rule: $\int x^n \,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ (for $n \neq -1$).
  • Constant Rule: $\int k \,dx = kx + C$.
  • Sum/Difference Rule: $\int (f(x) \pm g(x)) \,dx = \int f(x) \,dx \pm \int g(x) \,dx$.
  • Special Integrals: $\int \frac{1}{x} \,dx = \ln|x| + C$, and $\int e^x \,dx = e^x + C$.

Technique: u-Substitution

U-substitution is the reverse of the chain rule and is a powerful technique for simplifying integrals. The goal is to substitute part of the integrand with a variable $u$ to make it look like one of the basic integral forms.

If we have an integral of the form $\int f(g(x))g'(x) \,dx$, we can let $u = g(x)$, which means $du = g'(x) \,dx$. The integral becomes a much simpler:

$$ \int f(u) \,du $$

Applications of Integration

Integration has numerous applications beyond finding area, including:

  • Calculating the area between two curves.
  • Finding the volume of solids of revolution (e.g., using the disk or washer method).
  • Determining the average value of a function over an interval.
  • Solving separable differential equations.
