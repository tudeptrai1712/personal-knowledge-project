3D Vector Geometry 101

From lines and planes to angles in 3D space.

3D Coordinate Systems and Vectors

In a 3D Cartesian coordinate system, points are represented by an ordered triplet $(x, y, z)$. Vectors are often represented as an arrow starting at the origin and ending at a point $(x, y, z)$, denoted as $\vec{v} = \langle x, y, z \rangle$. The magnitude of a vector $\vec{v} = \langle x, y, z \rangle$ is its length from the origin to the point $(x, y, z)$, calculated as $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$.

The dot product of two vectors $\vec{a} = \langle a_1, a_2, a_3 \rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle b_1, b_2, b_3 \rangle$ is a scalar value calculated as:

$$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3 $$

The dot product is also related to the angle $\theta$ between the vectors by the formula:

$$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}| |\vec{b}| \cos(\theta) $$

This allows us to find the angle $\theta$ between two vectors: $\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}| |\vec{b}|}\right)$. If $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0$, the vectors are orthogonal (perpendicular).

The cross product of two vectors $\vec{a} = \langle a_1, a_2, a_3 \rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle b_1, b_2, b_3 \rangle$ is a new vector that is perpendicular to both $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$. Its direction is given by the right-hand rule, and its magnitude is $|\vec{a}| |\vec{b}| \sin(\theta)$. The cross product is calculated as:

$$ \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \langle a_2b_3 - a_3b_2, a_3b_1 - a_1b_3, a_1b_2 - a_2b_1 \rangle $$

The cross product is particularly useful for finding normal vectors to planes.

Equations of Lines and Planes

A line in 3D space is determined by a point $P_0(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ and a direction vector $\vec{v} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$. Its vector equation is $\vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{v}$, where $t$ is a scalar parameter.

A plane in 3D is defined by a point $P_0(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ and a normal vector $\vec{n} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$ that is orthogonal to the plane. Its scalar equation is:

$$ a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0 $$

Relationships Between Lines and Planes

We can analyze the relationship between a line (with direction $\vec{v}$) and a plane (with normal $\vec{n}$).

  • Intersection: The line and plane intersect at a single point. This occurs if the direction vector $\vec{v}$ is not orthogonal to the normal vector $\vec{n}$ (i.e., $\vec{v} \cdot \vec{n} \neq 0$). To find the point, substitute the line's parametric equations into the plane's equation and solve for the parameter $t$.
  • Parallelism: The line is parallel to the plane if its direction vector $\vec{v}$ is orthogonal to the plane's normal vector $\vec{n}$.

    $$ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{n} = 0 $$

    If a point on the line is also in the plane, the line lies entirely within the plane. Otherwise, it never intersects.

Angles in 3D Environments

The dot product is the key to finding angles between different geometric objects.

  • Angle Between Two Planes: The angle between two planes is the acute angle between their normal vectors, $\vec{n}_1$ and $\vec{n}_2$.

    $$ \theta = \arccos \left(\frac{|\vec{n}_1 \cdot \vec{n}_2|}{| \vec{n}_1| |\vec{n}_2|}\right) $$

  • Angle Between a Line and a Plane: This is the angle $\alpha$ between the line's direction vector $\vec{v}$ and its projection onto the plane. It is the complement of the angle $\theta$ between the line's direction vector $\vec{v}$ and the plane's normal vector $\vec{n}$.

    $$ \sin(\alpha) = \cos(\theta) = rac{|\vec{v} \cdot \vec{n}|}{|\vec{v}| |\vec{n}|} \implies \alpha = \arcsin \left(\frac{|\vec{v} \cdot \vec{n}|}{|\vec{v}| |\vec{n}|}\right) $$
